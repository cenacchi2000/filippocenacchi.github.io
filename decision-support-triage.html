Decision Support & Triage

A trust-calibrated triage system that layers multi-modal predictions and reinforcement-learning-style probing over diagnostic interviews, making model reasoning visible to clinicians.

From Scores to Decisions

Modern models can estimate depression and PTSD severity from speech, facial dynamics and text, but clinicians rarely see more than a single number and a threshold. This is misaligned with how triage decisions actually work: clinicians care about uncertainty, conflicting signals and which questions would help disambiguate the case.

This project treats triage as an interactive decision process rather than a static classification task. The system is designed not to replace clinicians but to act as a transparent, queryable copilot that helps them decide when to probe more, when to escalate and when to confidently reassure.

Architecture

The triage copilot ingests multi-modal features derived from interview data (E-DAIC / DAIC-WOZ style corpora) and outputs both severity estimates and calibrated uncertainty. On top of this, a probing policy, inspired by reinforcement learning, selects the most informative next question or modality to query, constrained by clinical safety rules.

Instead of asking “what is the diagnosis?”, the agent repeatedly asks “what should we ask or observe next to reduce uncertainty without overburdening the patient?”.

Layers:

• tri-modal fusion model (audio, face, text) with robustness to missing modalities
• uncertainty estimation and risk calibration
• RL-style probing module that chooses questions / modalities
• clinician dashboard that surfaces rationales and trajectories

Clinician-Facing Interface

On the surface, clinicians see a timeline of the interview with overlays showing when the model’s confidence rises or drops, which modalities are driving the estimate, and which potential next questions would have the largest expected reduction in uncertainty. Suggested probes are accompanied by short natural- language rationales rooted in model attributions.

Crucially, the interface exposes model humility: it indicates when the system is extrapolating beyond familiar regions of the data, when modalities are partially missing, and when it recommends immediate escalation regardless of uncertainty.

Deployment Targets

The triage copilot is designed to operate across several front-ends: humanoid robots such as Ameca, tele-health video calls and in-clinic screen-based questionnaires. In each case, it respects different bandwidth and workload constraints but shares the same underlying calibration and probing logic.

In the humanoid setting, it can quietly shape Ameca’s follow-up questions and pacing. In tele-health, it serves as a background assistant that highlights segments of concern and suggests follow-up questions to the clinician in real time or post-hoc.

Research Questions & Status

Ongoing work addresses questions such as how to balance exploration and burden when probing vulnerable participants, how to combine clinician feedback with data-driven policies, and how to report uncertainty in ways that align with clinical decision-making practices.

Prototype implementations have been validated offline on E-DAIC / DAIC-WOZ data, and are being integrated with the humanoid diagnostic agent and SimClinician environment for controlled user studies with clinicians.
