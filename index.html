Filippo Cenacchi

Doctoral Researcher in Computer Science at Frontier AI Research Centre at Macquarie University.

My research is at the intersection of humanoid robots, multimodal AI, and mental-health diagnostics. I design simulation-first pipelines, trust-calibrated interaction policies, and privacy-preserving avatarization to make early assessment more reliable and equitable. I am supervised by Distinguished Chair Professor Longbing Cao and Professor Deborah Richards, collaborating across computing, psychology, and clinical partners.

Research Focus

Humanoid-AI Diagnostic Agents

Designing conversational humanoid agents (e.g., Ameca) that sense and fuse facial, vocal, and linguistic cues to support early detection of depression, PTSD, and cognitive decline. Emphasis on trust calibration, safety, and clinician-style pacing.

Humanoid AI Dedicated project page →

Simulation-First Training

SimClinician: a simulation testbed with MetaHuman patients, synchronized speech/face/gaze and PHQ-8 / PCL-C flows. Enables counterfactual replay, nonverbal perturbations, and uncertainty-aware turn management.

Simulation Dedicated project page →

Privacy-Preserving Avatarization

Transforming raw sessions into re-playable 3D avatars that encode behavioral and affective signals while removing identifiers. Supports longitudinal tracking without storing voices or faces.

Avatarization Dedicated project page →

Decision Support & Triage

Interactive triage wizards and RL-inspired probing layered over E-DAIC and DAIC-WOZ to select targeted questions that maximize diagnostic clarity while exposing confidence and evidence.

Triage AI Dedicated project page →

Passive Dementia Screening

Language-free facial micro-dynamics pipeline for early dementia screening from in-the-wild talking-head video, using blink, gaze, and jaw dynamics with a calibrated, deployable risk score.

Dementia Dedicated project page →

MetaHuman Avatar Reel

Vertical demo of a MetaHuman avatar driven by Ameca-style signals. Showcases synchronised facial motion, gaze and pacing for simulation-first training.

Demo Reel Dedicated project page →

Invited Talks

21 Nov 2025
Can humanoid robots help diagnose cognitive and affective health?
School of Computing Industry Networking Event, School of Computing, Macquarie University
Invited talk presenting an Ameca-based diagnostic system that guides patients through natural conversations while analysing speech tone, rhythm, hesitation, and facial expressions. Sessions are converted into fully anonymised 3D avatars, and a multimodal fusion engine integrates these signals into early-warning profiles for anxiety and depression, aiming for more compassionate, precise, and scalable mental-health care.
