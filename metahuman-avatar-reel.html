Research Demo · 3D reconstruction, MetaHuman adaptation and synchronised embodiment

From Ultra-Realistic 3D Scan to MetaHuman, Voice Clone and Humanoid Embodiment

This project presents a full identity-preserving embodiment pipeline in which a real person is reconstructed from a high-detail 3D scan, translated into a photorealistic MetaHuman-compatible character, synchronised with cloned voice and animation, and finally connected across three coordinated embodiments: the physical humanoid AI embodiment, the MetaHuman agent embodiment and the original personal embodiment.

Research Aim

The scientific goal of this work is not merely visual realism, but the construction of a computationally aligned human embodiment stack. Starting from dense 3D capture, the pipeline progressively converts raw geometry into a semantically controllable and visually persuasive digital identity. This identity is then made operational through MetaHuman-based rigging, high-resolution material refinement, speech identity preservation and cross-platform behavioural synchronisation.

In academic terms, the contribution sits at the intersection of digital human reconstruction, animation retargeting, embodied conversational AI and human-centred telepresence. The key novelty is that the same identity can be instantiated in three synchronised conditions rather than being trapped in only one representational medium.

The pipeline therefore moves from capture, to cleanup, to registration, to MetaHuman adaptation, to 8K-grade rendering refinement, and finally to synchronised triadic embodiment, where a real person, a MetaHuman agent and a physical humanoid AI embodiment can all be aligned around the same identity model.

Academic Reconstruction Pipeline

The reconstruction process begins with a raw 3D scan that captures identity geometry at a level sufficient for facial proportions, local asymmetries and surface idiosyncrasies to be preserved. However, raw scans are not directly deployable for animation or embodied AI. They typically contain topological noise, incomplete regions, irregular boundaries and surface inconsistencies that must be normalised before any rigged digital-human system can meaningfully use them. The images below illustrate the full transition from dense scan data to the final operational digital identity.

1. Raw 3D identity acquisition

The first stage captures a dense geometric approximation of the subject. At this point, the scan preserves valuable craniofacial structure and broad surface morphology, but it still contains artefacts, incomplete contour regions and a representation that is unsuitable for animation, retargeting or semantic control.

2. Mesh inspection, topology diagnosis and geometric cleanup

The second stage focuses on structural diagnosis of the scan mesh. Dense triangulation, irregular edge flow and peripheral deformation are analysed so that geometry can be cleaned, reoriented and prepared for more stable downstream processing. This stage is essential because high realism without topological discipline leads to poor deformation and unreliable facial transfer.

3. Texture projection and canonical facial rendering

Once geometry becomes sufficiently stable, a canonical facial rendering can be produced by projecting appearance information back onto the reconstructed surface. This step converts the scan from a purely structural object into an identity-bearing visual asset, allowing skin detail, tonal variation and major facial characteristics to be preserved in a renderable form.

4. Landmark-guided semantic registration

Semantic facial landmarks are then aligned with the rendered face so that the subject can be mapped onto a controllable facial schema. This is the bridge between static likeness reconstruction and dynamic avatar control. Landmark-guided registration makes it possible to preserve identity while preparing the face for rig transfer, blendshape correspondence and expression-consistent retargeting.

5. MetaHuman adaptation and high-fidelity material refinement

After registration, the identity is adapted into a MetaHuman-compatible representation. In practical terms, this means matching the reconstructed person to a rigged digital human whose topology, facial control system and animation interfaces support real-time operation. Material refinement then pushes the asset toward highly realistic 8K-grade detail, particularly in skin response, pore-level variation, shading continuity and perceptual likeness.

6. Final photorealistic embodiment output

The final stage produces a stable, visually persuasive digital person rather than a mere scan-derived face. At this point, the character is no longer only an image asset: it becomes an operational embodied interface capable of receiving voice, animation and dialogue-control signals while still maintaining strong visual continuity with the original person.

Why the 2D Rendering Stage Matters

A crucial intermediate step in this workflow is the generation of a controlled 2D rendering from the reconstructed 3D identity. This stage may appear visually simple, but academically it is essential because it acts as the interface between raw scanned geometry and semantically interpretable facial representation. The 2D render enables identity checking, landmark placement, appearance correction, texture auditing and rig-transfer validation under a standardised viewpoint. In other words, it provides a stable observational plane from which the realism of the 3D subject can be evaluated and the adaptation to MetaHuman can be made more accurate and more reproducible.

Without this rendering stage, the transition from scan space to avatar space becomes much less controlled. With it, the workflow gains an explicit validation layer where likeness, feature alignment and material continuity can be iteratively corrected before animation and embodiment are introduced.

Synchronised Triadic Embodiment

The end result of the pipeline is not a single avatar, but a coordinated identity system spanning three embodiments that can interact with each other and with humans. This creates a new communicative configuration in which the same person can exist simultaneously as a physical agent, as a virtual photorealistic agent and as the original personal source identity.

Physical AI humanoid embodiment

In the first condition, the identity is projected into a physical humanoid platform such as Ameca. This embodiment contributes physical co-presence, spatial interaction, robotic gesture and situated engagement in the real world.

MetaHuman agent embodiment

In the second condition, the same identity is realised as a photorealistic MetaHuman agent. This embodiment contributes visual richness, cinematic realism, remote deployability and a controllable digital-human interface suitable for simulation, performance and telepresence.

Personal source embodiment

In the third condition, the original human source remains the reference identity for visual likeness, vocal style and behavioural grounding. This embodiment anchors the other two so that synchronisation remains identity-consistent rather than becoming a generic animation transfer.

When these three conditions are synchronised, the system moves beyond avatar generation and becomes a form of multi-embodiment identity orchestration. The person, the MetaHuman and the humanoid agent can all be linked through shared voice, aligned dialogue logic, coordinated turn-taking and matched expressive behaviour. This is the core reason the final demo is important: it illustrates not only realism, but the possibility of identity continuity across physical and virtual communicative bodies.

Voice Cloning and Behavioural Synchronisation

Visual embodiment alone is insufficient if the character does not sound and behave like the original identity. For that reason, the pipeline also includes voice cloning and synchronisation between rendered speech, agent timing and embodiment output. In the final demonstration, the vocal identity is preserved and aligned with the visual identity so that the audience perceives a coherent person rather than a disconnected animation layer.

When this vocal stream is synchronised with MetaHuman animation and, in parallel, with Ameca-based embodiment logic, the system produces a unified communicative agent that can be perceived across media as the same identity. This is precisely where the work becomes interesting from an HCI and embodied-AI perspective: the contribution is not only realism, but continuity of personhood cues across platforms.

In research and deployment contexts, this type of identity-preserving embodiment should be used with explicit consent, traceable provenance and clear disclosure of how voice, likeness and behavioural control are derived and synchronised.
