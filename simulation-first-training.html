Simulation-First Training for Diagnostic Agents

SimClinician is an Unreal Engine 5 / MetaHuman testbed that lets agents and clinicians rehearse thousands of synthetic interviews before anything reaches real patients.

Why Simulation-First?

Mental-health and cognitive screening tools are typically tuned directly on small, precious clinical datasets. Every design mistake carries ethical risk, and it is difficult to explore alternative interaction policies or edge cases without burdening participants. At the same time, large pre-training corpora rarely reflect the turn-taking, hesitation patterns and emotional nuance that appear in real clinical conversations.

SimClinician treats simulation not as a game-like toy, but as a high-fidelity rehearsal space where models, policies and clinicians can be stress-tested against a wide spectrum of synthetic patients before any deployment to humanoid robots or tele-health settings.

Core Design

The platform is built on Unreal Engine 5 with MetaHuman characters serving as simulated patients. Each scene binds together synchronised speech, facial animation, gaze behaviour and body posture, driven by annotated diagnostic flows such as PHQ-8 and PCL-C. Episodes are controlled by a scenario graph that encodes patient trajectories, risk profiles and possible branching responses.

A single scenario template can be replayed with many different non-verbal styles, noise conditions and pacing strategies, which is crucial for training robust multi-modal models and interaction policies.

Key ingredients:

• MetaHuman patients with controllable affect and engagement
• synchronised facial AUs, gaze and audio for each scripted turn
• environment variations (lighting, noise, camera framing)
• logging hooks that mirror what Ameca or tele-health systems would record

Counterfactual & Perturbation Experiments

SimClinician is explicitly optimised for counterfactual experiments. For a fixed transcript, the system can vary only the non-verbal profile of the patient (eye contact, micro-expressions, fidgeting), or only the clinician’s pacing and prompt wording, while keeping everything else identical. This makes it possible to isolate which behavioural dimensions most strongly influence a model’s risk estimates and a user’s sense of being understood.

These counterfactuals also underpin the design of humanoid-friendly behavioural cues: rather than simply copying human clinicians, policies are derived from structured sweeps over what works best for specific user profiles, noise levels and comorbidities.

Bridging to Real-World Data

SimClinician is not intended to replace clinical data. Instead, it sits between corpora such as E-DAIC / DAIC-WOZ and humanoid deployments. Models are first pre-trained on real datasets, then stress-tested and adapted within simulation, and finally evaluated again with clinicians and, eventually, patients.

This pipeline reduces the number of risky prototypes that need to be tested with vulnerable participants and allows more principled exploration of edge cases (for example, highly reticent or over-talkative patients) that are rare in existing datasets.

Current Status & Outlook

The current version of SimClinician supports small libraries of depression and trauma-focused scenarios, with export hooks for both offline model training and online policy evaluation. Next steps include richer social contexts (e.g., caregiver–patient dyads), explicit modelling of clinician workload, and direct streaming of simulated sessions into Ameca and other humanoids for hardware-in-the-loop testing.

A Concrete Study: Learning When to Ask

A recent study emerging from this line of work, Learning When to Ask: Simulation-Trained Humanoids for Mental-Health Diagnosis, makes the practical value of the SimClinician framework much more explicit. Rather than treating the simulator only as a visual demonstration environment, the study uses it as a structured training ground for diagnostic interaction policies, showing how a humanoid can learn not just what to ask, but also when to ask, when to wait, when to backchannel, and how to reduce interruption during sensitive mental-health screening exchanges. This is an important shift because clinically meaningful interaction quality depends not only on questionnaire coverage, but also on timing, pacing, rapport, and the ability to manage uncertainty without producing socially disruptive behaviour. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

The study operationalises this idea through an agent-centred, simulation-first pipeline in which interview data are transformed into 276 interactive Unreal Engine MetaHuman patients with synchronised speech, gaze, facial behaviour, and head–torso motion, coupled with structured PHQ-8 and PCL-C diagnostic flows. Within that environment, a perception–fusion–policy loop determines how the agent should proceed turn by turn: when to speak, when to offer brief acknowledgement, when to avoid cutting into the user, and when to probe further because the current evidence is still diagnostically ambiguous. Crucially, the paper frames these decisions under a safety-aware control layer, reinforcing the idea that interaction management in mental-health settings cannot be reduced to raw task completion alone. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

This connects directly to the broader logic of SimClinician presented on this page. Earlier sections describe the platform as a high-fidelity rehearsal space for counterfactual testing, multi-modal perturbation analysis, and safer progression toward embodied deployment. The study gives that vision a concrete methodological form: bounded nonverbal counterfactual replay is used during training, and an uncertainty-aware turn manager learns to seek clarification only when doing so is expected to reduce ambiguity in a controlled and socially appropriate way. In other words, the simulator is not merely generating synthetic scenes; it is being used to learn socially timed diagnostic behaviour that can later inform humanoid screening systems in the physical world. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3} :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

The reported results are also important for positioning the framework rigorously. The paper is explicit that the evaluation is simulation-only, with the humanoid treated as the transfer target rather than as a fully validated clinical endpoint. Within that scope, the authors compare three controllers and report that a customised TD3 approach outperforms PPO and CEM, achieving near-ceiling interview coverage while maintaining steadier pacing at comparable reward levels. The analysis further reports negligible turn overlap, better cut timing, fewer unnecessary clarification prompts, shorter waiting periods, and stable ranking patterns under modality dropout, renderer changes, and held-out patient splits. These findings matter because they suggest that the platform can support not only synthetic data generation, but also principled comparative evaluation of interaction policies before any higher-cost hardware studies are attempted. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

More broadly, this study helps clarify why simulation-first training is especially compelling for mental-health and cognitive assessment. Testing directly on hardware with real users is slow, expensive, and ethically restrictive, especially when iteration is required across conversational timing, trust-sensitive prompting, and subtle nonverbal dynamics. By contrast, a simulation-trained pipeline allows these design choices to be varied, stress-tested, and compared under controlled conditions before transfer to humanoid systems. In that sense, Learning When to Ask does not sit beside SimClinician as a separate project; it functions as one of the clearest demonstrations of the platform’s scientific purpose: using simulation to train and evaluate diagnostic agents whose behaviour is not only technically competent, but also interactionally disciplined and clinically usable. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}
