Humanoid-AI Diagnostic Agents

Building Ameca-driven agents that conduct structured yet naturalistic conversations and transform multimodal behavioural signals into early-warning indicators of depression, PTSD and cognitive decline.

Motivation

Conventional mental-health and cognitive screening procedures typically rely on brief, highly structured questionnaires administered in constrained clinical settings. Such instruments are difficult to repeat at high temporal resolution, capture only a narrow cross-section of behaviour, and are often insufficiently sensitive to subtle early changes in affect, speech, psychomotor function and interpersonal engagement. Conversely, fully automated digital agents often lack the social presence, accountability and embodied interaction required for high-stakes clinical assessment.

This project investigates whether a physically present humanoid robot can serve as an intermediate diagnostic interface: more consistent, scalable and continuously available than routine clinician-administered screening, while remaining more socially legible and trustworthy than disembodied software systems. The Ameca platform is therefore employed as an expressive front-end to a multimodal diagnostic engine and a trust-calibrated interaction policy.

System Overview

The humanoid-AI diagnostic agent is organised into four tightly coupled layers. Ameca’s microphones and visual sensors capture speech content, prosodic timing, hesitation phenomena, facial micro-dynamics and posture. A perception layer performs tri-modal feature extraction, feeding a fusion model derived from my severity-aware diagnostic work. A dialogue manager determines how to pace the interaction, when to probe, when to decelerate, and when to initiate clinician hand-off. Finally, a trust module surfaces calibrated risk estimates and explanatory evidence to both the patient and the supervising clinician.

Conceptually, Ameca is treated less as a “robot therapist” and more as an embodied diagnostic instrument with adaptive agency: it can modulate its interaction in order to elicit more informative behavioural data while maintaining a humane and clinically appropriate experience.

Current capabilities include:

• interview flows aligned with PHQ-8 / PCL-C and MoCA-style screening protocols
• fine-grained analysis of pauses, hesitations, self-corrections and speech-rate variability
• capture of facial micro-movements, gaze shifts and non-verbal response dynamics
• configurable “strictness” and empathy profiles for different patient populations

Motor Evaluation After Surgery

In addition to conversational and affective assessment, the broader framework can be extended to computer-vision-based motor evaluation in order to quantify postoperative recovery and functional impairment. This is particularly relevant in settings where repeated, objective measurement of movement quality is needed between clinical visits.

This example shows our computer-vision interface used to assess motor function following surgery through structured movement tasks and live pose-tracking feedback. The system estimates kinematic features such as joint excursion, inter-limb symmetry, movement smoothness, temporal coordination and task completion quality.

By extracting these features longitudinally, the framework can support the monitoring of strength, endurance and functional recovery, while also providing clinicians with quantitative indicators of rehabilitation progress. Such analyses may help identify persistent asymmetries, impaired movement strategies or deviations from expected recovery trajectories that warrant additional clinical attention.

Case 1 — Post-operative clinical synthesis

The patient was reviewed following repeat cervical decompressive surgery. Relative to the pre-operative state, the current profile is consistent with substantial improvement in the dominant pain complaint, in keeping with the original follow-up note. A residual distal arm pain component remains present. There is no evidence in the source note of a new neurological deficit, and the wound was described as clean and dry at review.

Within the monitored assessment, the principal residual abnormality was a protective upper-limb motor pattern affecting the symptomatic side. Left shoulder flexion was reduced by 49%, cervical rotation toward the affected side was reduced by 24%, and task initiation latency increased to 12 seconds during pain-relevant movement trials. Repeated overhead reach attempts showed a 29% reduction in peak movement velocity, earlier task termination on the left side, and visible unloading of the affected arm during the final phase of elevation.

Facial evidence remained consistent with residual pain-related effort. During arm elevation and cervical loading, brow lowering and periocular tightening were present for 21% of the active movement sequence, with three brief grimace-like responses and a peak facial-tension score 0.38 above the neutral baseline. Speech during symptom-focused questioning was mildly slowed and effortful. Mean pause duration increased to 1.5 seconds, total pause count rose to 8 during pain discussion, speech rate decreased from 141 to 116 words per minute, and vocal intensity fell by approximately 10% relative to neutral conversational segments.

Transcript-level language remained improvement-oriented but still symptom-specific. The patient repeatedly referred to the arm as “still sore” and described being “better than before” while avoiding strong catastrophic language. Pain-related terms appeared 10 times in the symptom segment, with emphasis placed on soreness, reduced confidence in lifting, and caution during shoulder use rather than on severe ongoing deterioration. The combined pattern of reduced left-sided shoulder excursion, delayed movement initiation, pain-linked facial tightening, and slower symptom-focused speech supports persistent but reduced postoperative symptom burden rather than complete symptom resolution.

Overall, the patient shows favourable post-operative recovery with measurable residual limitation still evident during left shoulder flexion, overhead reach, and cervical loading. The most appropriate interpretation is marked improvement with persistent pain-related protective behaviour rather than full functional normalisation. Continued physiotherapy and interval follow-up remain appropriate, with particular attention to whether reduced shoulder excursion, delayed initiation, and residual arm soreness continue to improve or become persistent features of recovery.

Case 2 — Clinical synthesis of lumbar and visual burden

The patient has documented high-grade L4–5 spinal canal stenosis with effacement of the cauda equina nerve roots, which constitutes a clear surgical pathology in the source note. The same record also documents concurrent cataracts and macular degeneration, with staged management prioritising ophthalmic treatment before spinal intervention. The source note explicitly states that the spinal condition was not regarded as a neurosurgical emergency at that review.

The monitored assessment demonstrated a functionally cautious lower-limb and postural profile. Sit-to-stand transfer time was prolonged to 5.8 seconds, turning time reached 4.6 seconds, mean step length was reduced by 21%, and trunk flexion during gait initiation remained limited. The patient required two brief rest pauses during sustained standing tasks, while postural sway increased by 18% during narrow-base stance compared with baseline standing segments. Transition phases were slowed and guarded, particularly when moving from seated to upright posture.

Visual findings were also clinically relevant. Fixation was interrupted nine times during guided target-following, three visual targets were missed on first presentation, and mean time to reacquire a target increased to 1.8 seconds. Gaze anchoring during instruction-following was intermittently unstable, particularly during seated-to-standing transitions and when the task required sustained visual fixation. Facial evidence suggested visible effort and discomfort during loading tasks. Brow tension and mid-face tightening increased during sit-to-stand and turning sequences, with visible strain responses present for 22% of the active mobility interval and a peak facial-tension score 0.37 above the patient’s neutral baseline.

Speech during symptom-related questioning was slower and more effortful than during neutral conversation. Response latency increased to 2.1 seconds, mean pause duration reached 1.8 seconds, total pause count rose to 11 in mobility-related segments, and speech rate fell to 104 words per minute. Vocal energy also declined modestly during discussion of standing tolerance, back discomfort, and everyday functional limitation. Transcript-level language centred on functional caution and staged decision-making. Repeated reference was made to difficulty standing for prolonged periods, the need to be careful with movement, fear of aggravating symptoms, and the importance of addressing visual problems first. Terms related to “standing,” “back,” “eyes,” “careful,” and “worse” appeared repeatedly, indicating an organised but burden-aware symptom narrative rather than diffuse distress.

The combined pattern of guarded transfers, slowed gait-related behaviour, fixation instability, facial effort during loading, and slower symptom-focused speech supports a clinically meaningful functional burden that extends beyond the structural diagnosis statement alone. Overall, the patient demonstrates functionally significant limitation consistent with the documented lumbar pathology, with additional observable visual instability likely contributing to task-performance difficulty. This supports staged management with close follow-up while also providing a useful behavioural baseline against which future changes in gait confidence, standing tolerance, gaze stability, and task hesitancy can be compared over time.

Interaction & Trust Calibration

A central research question concerns how much autonomy the robot should exercise in shaping the interview. The interaction policy therefore exposes a set of explicit control dimensions:

• Risk-aware pacing – sessions begin with low-sensitivity prompts and only escalate when early signals are stable and the participant’s behaviour indicates sufficient comfort and engagement.
• Evidence-linked feedback – rather than presenting opaque scores, the system highlights which segments of speech and which non-verbal patterns contribute most strongly to a given risk estimate.
• Hand-off triggers – thresholds specifying when Ameca should suspend probing and defer to a human clinician are encoded directly in the policy.

These mechanisms are evaluated not only in terms of predictive performance, but also with respect to whether users perceive the system as competent, honest and appropriately cautious in a clinical context.

Relationship to Other Projects

This work builds directly on my broader programme in multimodal diagnosis and trust-calibrated AI. The diagnostic models extend methods developed for severity-aware fusion of speech, facial behaviour and language. The interaction-policy component connects to my interest in explainability, uncertainty communication and the appropriate distribution of agency between humans and machine systems in sensitive decision settings.

More broadly, the project contributes to an emerging view of embodied AI in healthcare: not as a replacement for clinicians, but as a socially situated interface that can improve the granularity, accessibility and repeatability of diagnostic assessment.
