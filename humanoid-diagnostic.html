What You Can Explore in the Demo

SimClinician is organised around the full journey a psychologist would take when negotiating AI suggestions: from baseline questionnaire scores to multimodal evidence panels, risk estimates and final decisions. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

In the interface you can:

• Start from validated anchors such as PHQ-8 and PCL-C, grounding decisions in measurement-based care.
• Inspect high-resolution spectrograms with rails for flat prosody, silence and stress bursts.
• Scroll through transcript ribbons showing negations, hedging, sentiment shifts and temporal focus, then drill down into theory-aligned quotes.
• Replay de-identified avatars that mirror facial action units and gaze, protecting privacy while keeping non-verbal cues analysable. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Decision layer & policies

At the bottom of each case the banded risk gauge aggregates depression (0–4) and PTSD (0–2) into a fused risk score, surfaced with plain-language next-step suggestions. The action bar lets a “synthetic clinician” confirm, override up or down, or defer, with soft frictions that model real-world confirmation and alert-fatigue effects. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Under the hood, a unified controller applies consistent policies in both interactive and large-scale simulation runs, so what you see in the demo is exactly what is used to generate judge–advisor statistics, calibration curves and override distributions.

Why Simulation First?

Live clinician studies are expensive, slow to approve and ethically sensitive. SimClinician acts as a buffer: it allows thousands of simulated psychologist–AI interactions to be run on top of real interviews before any clinician is recruited. This makes it possible to tune interface levers such as explanation granularity, override friction and collaboration style (safety vs. parsimony vs. deferral) while directly measuring their impact on acceptance, escalation and latency. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

The demo you see here is not a toy mock-up: it is the same UI exercised by the simulation harness, with a shared controller and logging schema. Design tweaks explored in the browser can be swept at scale to understand how they reshape clinician–AI collaboration patterns.

Status & Next Directions

The current prototype runs on interviews from the E-DAIC corpus, generating calibrated risk estimates and full judge–advisor logs across thousands of simulated sessions. Ongoing work focuses on extending the same controller to embodied settings such as humanoid robots, expanding cognitive-screening tasks, and connecting simulation-derived policies to small-scale clinician pilots and longitudinal measurement-based care loops. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}
